Tooth Eruption

Introduction

Tooth eruption is the physiological process by which a developing tooth moves from its position within the alveolar bone into the oral cavity until it reaches functional occlusion. It is a complex, well-coordinated biological process involving bone remodeling, periodontal ligament activity, and root development.

Phases of Tooth Eruption

1. Pre-Eruptive Phase

  • Occurs during crown formation.
  • Tooth germ moves within jaw bones.
  • Adjusts position due to jaw growth.

2. Eruptive (Prefunctional) Phase

  • Begins with root formation.
  • Tooth moves through bone and gingiva.
  • Ends when tooth reaches occlusion.

3. Post-Eruptive (Functional) Phase

  • Maintains tooth position.
  • Compensates for jaw growth.
  • Adjusts for occlusal wear.

Mechanisms of Tooth Eruption

  • Bone resorption above erupting tooth by osteoclasts.
  • Bone formation below tooth.
  • Periodontal ligament traction forces.
  • Root elongation contributes to movement.
  • Hydrostatic pressure in pulp tissues.

Why Tooth Eruption Does NOT Cause Bleeding

  • Eruption is slow and controlled.
  • Minimal trauma to blood vessels.
  • Gradual separation of gingival tissues.
  • Keratinized epithelium protects underlying capillaries.
  • Inflammation is mild, not hemorrhagic.

Clinical Considerations

  • Teething discomfort due to pressure and inflammation.
  • Eruption cysts may occur.
  • Delayed eruption may indicate systemic conditions.
  • Poor oral hygiene may cause gingival bleeding (not eruption itself).

Primary teeth erupt between 6 months and 3 years. Permanent teeth erupt between 6 and 21 years. The process is genetically programmed and hormonally regulated.

Dental follicle signaling molecules regulate osteoclast recruitment, which is essential for bone resorption during eruption.
